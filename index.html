The Wall

Generally, it is believed that “de Waal Straat” was originally an earthen wall delineating the northern boundary of the New Amsterdam settlement. Wall Street then morphed into a marketplace (which sold slaves legally in 1711), before becoming the scene for George Washington’s inauguration in 1789. The founding fathers also passed the Bill of Rights on Wall Street. Later, it became synonymous with the Financial District, fat cats, and the stock exchange. Remember the 1929 crash?

Another theory for Wall Street’s name is that it was named after Walloons, possibly a Dutch abbreviation for Walloon being Waal. There were 30 Walloon families among the first North American settlers in 1624.

More than 25% of the world’s gold is located 80 feet beneath the Federal Reserve Bank on Wall Street.

A Melting Pot

“The city is like poetry; it compresses all life, all races and breeds, into a small island and adds music and the accompaniment of internal engines.” – E.B. White

Scroll

The term “melting pot” came into use after 1908 and the premiere of Israel Zangwill’s play The Melting Pot.

New York meshes people from all walks of life, nationalities and ethnicities together. As many as 800 languages are spoken in the city, and 36% of the population is foreign-born.

1625: The Birth of New York City with Manhattan settlement

The first Dutch people settled on Governor’s Island before migrating to Manhattan. In 1626, the New Amsterdam colony paid Native Americans 60 guilders worth of trade goods for the entire Manhattan island. This amounts to about 24 modern-day dollars.

The name “Manhattan” comes from the original Lenape name of the island, “Mannahatta”, which may have meant “island of many hills”.

In 1664 the English conquered the Dutch colonies, and New Amsterdam became New York.

The Grid

“New York is the biggest collection of villages in the world.” – Alistair Cooke

New York’s famous grid plan was formulated in the Commissioners’ Plan of 1811.

The area above Houston Street was conceived of as long avenues with orthogonal streets, a grid that defines Manhattan today. The commission that created described it as combining “beauty, order and convenience”. It is estimated that 40% of buildings that already existed above Houston Street were moved to fit within the new urban plan.

Race to the Top

Scroll

New York is a city of skyscrapers. There are currently 5,485 high-rises in the city.

The history of skyscrapers in New York City began when the World Building, commissioned by Joseph Pulitzer, was completed in 1890. This was the first building to surpass the 284 ft (87 m) spire of Trinity Church. (The building was later demolished in 1955 to accommodate an expanded entrance to the Brooklyn Bridge).

From the 1910s to the 1930s, New York entered into a high-rise construction boom, racing to the heights unimaginable for buildings. 16 of the city’s tallest buildings were built during this time including the Woolworth Building, Bank of Manhattan Trust, Chrysler Building and the Empire State Building.

The tallest completed building in the city is the Empire State Building, finished in 1931. It rises 1,250 ft (381 m), and with its antenna its height is increased to 1,454 ft (443 m). The Empire State building held the World’s Tallest Building” record until 1972. During construction, it competed with 40 Wall Street, and the Chrysler Building for title of “World’s Tallest Building” and ultimately surpassed their height upon completion.

Construction of the building only took 15 months, a record 410 days.

Every year, the Empire State Building has hosted a Run-up, where participants compete in a foot race from the ground level to the observation deck on the 86th floor. The record time is 9 minutes and 33 seconds, achieved by Australian cyclist Paul Crake.

The Crossroads of the World

Scroll

Times Square is the most-visited tourist destination in New York, fielding over 39 million tourists each year. Approximately 300,000 visit every day. There are approximately 50 super signs in Times Square area, 40 theatres which includes 22 landmark Broadway theatres.

Times Square was once known as “Longacre Square”, but was renamed in 1904 to its trademark title when The New York Times moved into the Times Building, now called One Times Square. Other nicknames of the square include “The Tenderloin”, “Crossroads of the World” and “The Great White Way”. In the 1920s, it was considered the most desirable location in New York.

Once the stock market crashed in 1929, Times Square experienced an exodus of businesses, which made room for seedier organizations. It became an area of peep shows and prostitutes. It wasn’t until the 1990s that Mayor Giuliani made a push for cleaning up Times Square. This process was called the “Disneyification” of Times Square.

Nowadays, close to one million people congregate at Times Square to watch the ball drop ever new year.

The Beach Pneumatic

In 1867, the Beach Pneumatic Transit Company secretly constructed a 95 m long subway system under Broadway to demonstrate the possibilities of underground transport. Boss Tweed ultimately shut the project down as he was funding an elevated rail system.

The Beach Pneumatic Transit System was based upon technologies similar to the pneumatic tube system which allowed businesses, banks, and post offices to quickly transport documents and materials throughout a building.

The first subway line in New York City opened in 1904; a response to the transportation gridlock caused by the Great Blizzard of 1888.

A Rocky Past

The 1980s can be summarized as the “Jekyll and Hyde” period of the New York Subway System. As the decade began, it had the filthiest trains, craziest graffiti, noisiest wheels, and the weirdest, strangest passengers. When the decade wrapped up, there were already cleaner trains, no graffiti and quieter wheels.

1989 ended with the threat of a large operating deficit and the threat of a fare increase was raised to pay for it. Just as they did in 2013 and have planned for 2015.
